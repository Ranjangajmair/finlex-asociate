Hydropower Project Development in Nepall

This article discusses the legal framework for hydropower projects in Nepal, including relevant laws, licensing modes, and incentives for development.

1. Legal Framework for Hydropower

The main legislation governing hydropower in Nepal includes:


Legislation

Scope

Electricity Act, 1992 (2049) ("Electricity Act") & Electricity Rules, 1993 (2050) ("Electricity Rules")

Primarily focuses on the licensing of electricity projects.

Electricity Regulatory Commission Act, 2017 (2074)

Establishes the Electricity Regulatory Commission for effective management of electricity production, transmission, distribution, and trade.

Nepal Electricity Authority Act, 1984 (2041)

Governs the Nepal Electricity Authority, which manages power supply.

Public Private Partnership and Investment Act, 2019 (2075)

Regulates projects over 200 MW capacity or those with an investment exceeding 6 billion Nepalese Rupees.

2. Governing Authorities

Below are the key authorities responsible for hydropower project development and their functions:


Authority

Functions / Responsibilities

Department of Electricity Development (DOED)

Main regulatory authority for electricity projects; grants, renews, amends, and revokes licenses.

Investment Board of Nepal (IBN)

Requires investment approval for projects over 200 MW and exceeding 6 billion Nepalese Rupees.

Electricity Regulatory Commission (ERC)

Manages the production, transmission, distribution, and trade of electricity.

3. Modes of Hydropower Development

Hydropower projects in Nepal are typically developed under a Build Own Operate and Transfer (BOOT) model, with three primary modes:

Mode 1: License Application (Section 3 of the Electricity Act)

Mode 2: Directly enter into an agreement with Government of Nepal

Mode 3: Competitive Bidding Process


Mode 1: License Application (Section 3 of the Electricity Act)

The licensing process involves two stages:

  1. Survey License: This initial license allows for feasibility and environmental studies.

  2. Generation/Transmission/Distribution License: If the project is deemed feasible, separate licenses are issued for generation, transmission, and distribution.

License Duration


License Type

Duration

Survey License

5 Years

Generation / Transmission / Distribution License

Mode 2: Agreement with the Government (Section 35 of Electricity Act)

Under this mode, a party can generate, transmit, or distribute electricity by entering into an agreement with the Government of Nepal. However, the exact rights and scope under Section 35 are not clearly defined. The government usually calls for competitive bidding for such projects.

Mode 3: Competitive Bidding

A company can acquire a project through the competitive bidding process. This process applies to:

  • Projects studied by the Government of Nepal or its institutions.

  • Projects with canceled licenses due to issues like non-fulfillment of conditions or financial closure.

Interested companies submit applications to the Ministry of Energy via DOED. The approval is based on competitive bids.

4. Development Steps for Hydropower Projects

The typical steps involved in hydropower project development in Nepal are as follows:

  1. Issuance of Survey License by DOED.

  2. Conducting feasibility and environmental studies, along with executing a Power Purchase Agreement (PPA).

  3. Application for Generation License to DOED.

  4. Financial closure within one year of the Generation License issuance.

  5. Securing all necessary approvals.

  6. Construction of the project.

  7. Project operation (up to 50 years).

  8. Handover of the project after license expiration.

5. License Expiry and Ownership Transfer

The maximum duration for a hydropower project license is 50 years, though often granted for 35 years. Upon expiration, ownership must be transferred to the Government of Nepal. Developers may continue operating under a new agreement with the government.

6. Non-nationalization During License Period

During the license period, the Government of Nepal cannot nationalize any land, buildings, equipment, or structures related to the hydropower project. 

7. Electricity Sale

Electricity generated can be sold locally or exported. Sale can occur by:

  • Connecting to the national grid.

  • Operating independently without the grid.

8. NEA as the Sole Buyer

In practice, the Nepal Electricity Authority (NEA) is the exclusive buyer of electricity. Export agreements with the government are required for any international sales.

9. Foreign Investment

The hydropower sector allows for 100% foreign investment. However, projects often offer shares to local communities and the public.

10. Fiscal Incentives for Hydropower Projects

Hydropower projects in Nepal enjoy various fiscal incentives:


Tax Heading

Incentives

Corporate Tax

20%

Income Tax Holiday

100% exemption for the first 10 years; 50% for the next 5 years; an additional 15% concession for public companies

Tax Benefits

100% VAT exemption on machinery imports; 0% VAT on transactions of machinery

Custom Benefits

1% customs duty on construction equipment imports

Carrying Forward of Losses

Losses can be carried forward for up to 12 years.
